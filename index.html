About Me

I turn messy data into
things that matter.

Hello, I'm Kavya Sri Yakkala! Since I was a kid, I've always noticed problems around me and wondered how they could be solved. That curiosity eventually led me to computer science during my undergraduate studies, where I explored areas ranging from cybersecurity and IoT to machine learning. Over time, I realized that data was the place where my curiosity truly found a home.

A turning point for me came during my final year, when I interned at Silicon Labs. There, I worked on embedded systems and automated CI/CD pipelines for production software. It was my first real experience building systems that move beyond code on a screen and into real-world use, and it showed me how powerful well-engineered technology can be.

Today, I am a graduate student at NYU studying Data Science at the Courant Institute. I also work as a Graduate Research Assistant at NYU Steinhardt in the Department of Applied Psychology, where I develop machine learning pipelines that analyze wearable sensor data to study patterns related to children's behavior and attention, and use those insights to better understand future behavioral outcomes. It's the kind of problem where the data is messy, the signals are subtle, and the insights can actually matter.

That's the kind of work I'm drawn to — building intelligent systems that turn real-world data into understanding, and sometimes into something that makes life a little better. I care about building data-driven products end-to-end — from defining the right problems to making tradeoffs and driving measurable impact — not just optimizing models in isolation.

NYU Steinhardt
Graduate Research Assistant
Dept. of Applied Psychology

I am working with my team on longitudinal wearable data from a national pediatric cohort — weeks of raw actigraphy signals collected from children in real-world settings. The data is messy and incomplete, so much of the current work focuses on preprocessing: cleaning gaps in the time series, normalizing multi-week activity signals, and engineering features related to movement intensity, sleep timing, and circadian rhythms.

From there, we will build and validate autoregressive and supervised machine learning classification pipelines that use these features to study patterns related to children's behavior and attention, with the goal of understanding how early activity signals may relate to future behavioral outcomes.
